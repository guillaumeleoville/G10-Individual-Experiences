Understanding the framework

What Is
GRIT?

GRIT is a concept developed by psychologist Dr. Angela Duckworth. It combines passion and perseverance — the willingness to keep going toward a goal even when it's uncomfortable, boring, or difficult. It's the same whether you're doing yoga or boxing or orienteering. The four pillars below are what you're assessed on.

Pillar A
Physical Interest
Being motivated to try new challenges in your activity, staying curious about what you're learning, and showing up with genuine enthusiasm — even when the session looks tough.
e.g. "I was eager to try the BINGO workout even though I hadn't done it before." e.g. "I was keen to try the more advanced balance poses even when they looked difficult." e.g. "I wanted to learn the combination sequences even though coordination felt hard." e.g. "I was enthusiastic about trying a new partner balance even though it felt risky." e.g. "I was curious about reading contour lines even though map work was new to me."
Pillar B
Physical Effort
Actually working hard. Maintaining a high level of active participation. Not stopping or giving up when your activity gets uncomfortable or technically difficult. Completing every task fully.
e.g. "During Tabata I wanted to stop at round 6 but I pushed through all 8 rounds." e.g. "I held the warrior pose even when my legs were shaking instead of coming out early." e.g. "I kept my guard up through the whole pad round even when my arms were burning." e.g. "I attempted the shoulder stand three times despite falling, until I held it." e.g. "I ran the full course even when I was tired and had already found most controls."
Pillar C
Academic Interest
Being genuinely curious about GRIT as a concept. Engaging with the theory, watching the TED talk, and thinking about how it applies beyond PE — to your other subjects, sport, or life.
e.g. "I connected GRIT to how I approach difficult runs outside school." e.g. "I made a connection between GRIT and staying focused during exam stress." e.g. "I connected the discipline in boxing drills to how I approach hard homework." e.g. "I realised that trusting my partner required the same GRIT as trusting a process." e.g. "I connected orienteering problem-solving to how I handle mistakes in other areas."
Pillar D
Academic Effort
Completing your slides and journal entries on time. Staying engaged so you understand new concepts. Writing reflections that are honest and specific — not vague. Submitting everything when it's due.
e.g. "I completed Journal Entry 1 after Lesson 4 with specific examples from that week." e.g. "I completed all slides on time and described specific poses that challenged me." e.g. "My journal entry named specific combinations I struggled with and what I tried." e.g. "I wrote about a specific moment of failure in a balance and what I did next." e.g. "I described a specific navigation error I made and the strategy I used to fix it."

Your 8 sessions

How Your
Unit Runs

All groups follow the same overall structure. Your activity-specific skills and exercises will be introduced by your teacher. The milestones — GRIT scores, journal entry, final reflection — are the same for everyone.

B
Before Lesson 1 — In the Classroom Slides 1–5 due
Before your first practical session, complete slides 1–5 on your student slides. This includes: writing your own definition of GRIT, watching Dr. Duckworth's TED Talk, completing your pre-unit GRIT score in the spreadsheet, and writing a paragraph about your strengths and areas for growth.
1
Lesson 1 — Introduction to Your Activity
Your teacher introduces the essential skills and knowledge for your chosen activity. You'll learn the objectives for the unit and what you'll be working towards. First impressions matter — bring energy and curiosity. Yoga: introductory poses, breathing and foundational sequences. Boxing: stance, footwork, basic jab and cross technique. Acro Gymnastics: safety principles, basic partner balances, trust exercises. Orienteering: map reading basics, compass use, campus route 1.
2
Lesson 2 — Building Skills
Continue developing the core skills of your activity. Your teacher will build on what was introduced in Lesson 1. Focus on technique and understanding — not just going through the motions. Yoga: building on foundational poses, introducing a linked flow. Boxing: adding the cross, practising jab-cross combinations on pads. Acro Gymnastics: developing weight-sharing, introducing new base positions. Orienteering: cone-bearing and distance exercises, reading contour lines.
3
Lesson 3 — Developing Confidence
Skills development continues. You should be starting to feel more comfortable with the fundamentals. This is where GRIT starts to show up — some things will feel harder than expected. Notice that feeling and keep going. Yoga: longer holds, building strength and balance in more challenging poses. Boxing: footwork patterns, defensive movement, adding the hook. Acro Gymnastics: progressing to more advanced balances, group work begins. Orienteering: off-campus route introduction, applying compass skills outdoors.
4
Lesson 4 — Challenge + Journal Entry 1 Journal Entry 1 due
Skills and knowledge continue. At the end of this lesson you'll write Journal Entry 1 (slide 7): What challenges are you facing so far? Which strategies are you using to overcome them? Write while it's fresh — you'll use this in your final reflection. Yoga: independent practice — attempt a full short sequence on your own. Boxing: first full pad round using combinations learned so far. Acro Gymnastics: small group sequence challenge — plan and perform a short routine. Orienteering: group challenge course — apply map and compass together.
5
Lesson 5 — Progression
Your teacher introduces more complex or challenging elements of your activity. By now you have a base — it's time to push further. Ask yourself honestly which GRIT pillar you've been strongest and weakest in so far. Yoga: introducing inversions or more advanced balancing poses. Boxing: extended combinations, introducing slips and defensive responses. Acro Gymnastics: more complex transitions, developing the group sequence further. Orienteering: capture the flag orienteering — navigation under competitive pressure.
6
Lesson 6 — Pushing Further
Two thirds of the unit done. Your teacher continues to build on skills and knowledge. This is where the gap between those who engaged early and those who held back starts to show. Which side are you on? Yoga: full flow practice, refining technique and breath connection. Boxing: sparring-style partner work or mitt combinations at pace. Acro Gymnastics: polishing the group sequence, adding transitions and timing. Orienteering: solo or pair route behind Waldlust U-Bahn — real-world navigation.
7
Lesson 7 — Personal Challenge Personal benchmark recorded
A personal challenge or benchmark attempt in your activity. Your teacher will record your performance — this gives you a concrete data point to write about in your summative reflection. Give it everything. Yoga: attempt a more advanced pose or hold a full flow sequence independently. Boxing: demonstrate a full combination sequence or pad round. Acro: attempt a more complex balance or perform your group sequence. Orienteering: complete a timed course solo, applying all skills learned.
8
Lesson 8 — Final Session + Post-Unit Reflection Post-unit GRIT + Slides 8–11
Your final practical session. At the end, you'll complete your post-unit GRIT score and finish slides 8–11. Compare your pre and post scores, reflect on how GRIT showed up in your activity, and answer: how will you apply this in the future? This is your summative assessment. Yoga: final independent flow performance, then post-unit reflection. Boxing: final combination showcase or fitness round, then reflection. Acro Gymnastics: final group sequence performance, then reflection. Orienteering: geocaching route or final navigation challenge, then reflection.

How you'll be marked

Your
Assessment

The assessment is identical for all five activity groups. Your mark is not about which activity you chose — it's about how you engaged with GRIT through that activity.

Formative Assessment

Ongoing

Formative assessment happens throughout the unit. It's your teacher observing your effort, engagement and progress lesson by lesson — in your specific activity and in your academic work.

What your teacher is watching for:
Pre-unit GRIT score and reflection (slides 1–5) · Your effort and participation each session · Journal Entry 1 after Lesson 4 · Whether your behaviour in lessons matches what you say about yourself in your writing

Summative Assessment

End of Unit

Your summative mark comes from your end-of-unit reflective work — slides 8–11. This is where you compare your pre and post GRIT scores, reflect on how GRIT showed up in your activity, and explain what changed.

What makes a strong summative submission:
Specific examples from named lessons in your activity · Honest comparison of scores · A real, credible answer to "How will you apply GRIT in the future?" — not just "I will try harder"
How to do well

Tips For
Success

01
Notice your moments of wanting to quit. That exact moment — whether it's a hard yoga pose, a tough boxing round, or a confusing map — is where this unit lives. What you do next is your data.
02
Be specific in your writing. "I tried hard" scores Not Yet. "In Lesson 4 during the partner burpee drilltree posethe combination sequencethe shoulder standthe compass challenge, I kept going because..." scores much higher.
03
Your personal benchmark is your evidence. The performance your teacher records in Lesson 7 gives you something concrete to write about. Don't hold back — give it everything.
04
Don't leave the slides until the last minute. Your Journal Entry 1 must be written right after Lesson 4, while the experience is fresh. Retroactive reflection is always weaker.
05
After every session, ask yourself: "Which GRIT pillar was I in today?" That habit — whether you're doing yoga or boxing or orienteering — will make your summative writing much easier and much stronger.
06
Connect GRIT beyond PE. The strongest reflections make links to other subjects, sport, music, relationships — anywhere perseverance shows up. That's what Exceeding looks like.
Common questions

FAQs

Can I change my activity? +
No — you made your choice before the unit started. The whole unit is designed around you committing to one activity for 8 lessons and reflecting on that experience. Changing halfway would undermine both your progress and your ability to write a strong summative reflection. If you have a serious concern, speak to your teacher privately.
Does it matter which activity I chose? Will some be easier to score well in? +
No — the assessment is identical for all five groups and is not about physical skill level. It's about GRIT. A student who struggles in yoga but reflects deeply and honestly on that struggle can score just as highly as an experienced gymnast in acro. What matters is how you engage with difficulty and how well you articulate that in your writing.
What if I miss a lesson? +
You are responsible for catching up. Speak to your teacher before the next lesson. Missing a lesson doesn't excuse you from the slides or journal entries — but it does make your summative writing harder, because you'll have less experience to draw from. Attend every session you can.
How long should my journal entries and reflections be? +
Length is less important than depth. A focused paragraph with specific examples is worth far more than two vague pages. Each reflection prompt has specific questions — make sure you answer all of them. For the post-unit reflection (slide 10), aim for at least one full paragraph that covers all four questions.
What if my GRIT score goes down from pre to post? +
That's completely fine — as long as you reflect on it honestly. The assessment is not about having a higher score. It's about your ability to understand GRIT, recognise where you are with it, and explain why. A lower score with an insightful explanation can score higher than a higher score with no real reflection.
When exactly are the slides due? +
Slides 1–5 must be completed before Lesson 1 starts. Journal Entry 1 (slide 7) is due after Lesson 4 — complete it at the end of that class or that same evening. The post-unit GRIT score and slides 8–11 are completed at the end of Lesson 8. Your teacher will confirm any specific submission deadlines.
